PhD Economist | AI Behavioral Research | Causal Inference | Claremont Graduate University

About

I study how large language models reason, decide, and take risks. My dissertation shows that system prompts act as cognitive switches, flipping the same model between deliberative reasoning and textbook retrieval with measurable behavioral consequences. "Consider your values" produces 0% defection in game-theoretic experiments. "Make your choice now" produces 83%. The model doesn't change. The prompt does.

This finding has direct implications for measuring AI's economic impact. Any assessment of what AI "can do" for a given occupation depends on how the model is prompted, making aggregate benchmarks unreliable proxies for real-world capability.

I also build the infrastructure my research requires. I wrote a geocoder in Python and Rust that processes 150 million police stop records at 280 requests per second, replacing a vendor solution and saving $200K a year. I built the full experimental pipeline for my LLM behavioral studies from scratch: prompt generation, API integration, response scoring, and analysis across 31,638 observations.

Before economics, I spent four years as a software engineer at SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory building data acquisition systems and publishing six peer-reviewed papers on silicon detector R&D. My degrees span physics (UCSC), mathematics (CGU), and economics (CGU), and I approach LLM behavior questions with a quantitative toolkit that draws on all three.

I'd rather build the tool I need than wait for someone else to make it.
